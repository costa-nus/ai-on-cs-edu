Total enrollment vs new enrollment
Two views of the GPT effect on CS programs

Stock measures (total enrolled) change slowly. Flow measures (newly entered each year) react quickly. Separating them avoids the apples-to-oranges problem on a single axis. ChatGPT launched November 2022 — the marker falls between AY 2021—22 and AY 2022—23.

⚠ Note on master's data

The CRA Taulbee Survey reports master's enrollment in Tables M5 (new master's) and M6 (total master's enrollment), but I was unable to fully extract those specific table contents from the source PDFs in this session — they appear later in the documents than the portions retrievable through my current fetch tooling. Rather than fabricate numbers, the master's series is shown as "data not extracted" in the tables below and is omitted from the chart lines. Bachelor's and PhD data are fully verified.

What the two figures together reveal

Bachelor's: Both stock and flow are climbing strongly. New BS majors grew faster (+37% over 4 years) than total enrollment (+22%), meaning future bachelor's classes will be even bigger as these new admits work through the 4-year pipeline.

PhD: The two views tell different stories. Stock (total enrolled) shows smooth +8% growth. Flow (new admits) shows the COVID dip in '20—21 (–14.6%), partial recovery, then a sharp +13.1% jump in '23—24 — the first full academic year that began after ChatGPT. This is the clearest "GPT effect" visible in the data: it shows up in new entrants, not yet in totals.

The general lesson: Flows lead, stocks follow. If the AI boom is reshaping CS education, expect the visible signal to appear in new-enrollment metrics first, with total enrollment rising over the next 4–5 years as those cohorts graduate through.
