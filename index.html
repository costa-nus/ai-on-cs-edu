How did the ChatGPT moment change U.S. computer science education?

By
  • Junyuan Hong
  • Claude Opus 4.7

A investigation on U.S. bachelor's CS enrollment, three academic years after ChatGPT's November 2022 debut.

▌ The numbers at a glance
  • GPT did not change the national trend: bachelor's CS enrollment had already grown ~60% over the seven years before ChatGPT, and the post-GPT year sits squarely on that pre-existing line.
  • The first real decline shows up in AY 2025—26, three years after the GPT trigger: bachelor's enrollment peaked at most tracked schools around 2024, then rolled over — Georgia Tech −18%, Stony Brook −15% from peak.
“Because I think computer science may well not be a growth industry going forward. And I’m not sure I would recommend 18 year olds to major in computer science.”
— Mike Stonebraker (Turing Award winner), interview on Apr, 2026

Stonebraker's remark cuts against a decade of conventional wisdom about CS as a guaranteed growth field. If a Turing laureate is now hesitant to recommend the major, the question is empirical: has the post-ChatGPT generation actually started turning away from CS? This blog tracks U.S. CS enrollment using CRA Taulbee Survey and data reported by universities to investigate the trends.

The wave started long before ChatGPT. Bachelor's CS enrollment climbed from 114,607 in AY 2015—16 to 182,973 in AY 2022—23 — a steady +60% over seven years, with the post-GPT year sitting squarely on the pre-existing line. New bachelor's majors had already crossed 35,000 in AY 2018—19 (35,245), well before ChatGPT, dipped through COVID, then re-broke that ceiling in AY 2022—23 (43,993). Source: CRA Taulbee Survey, Table 1 [S8–S11, S1–S4].

Recovery, not revolution. New PhD admissions grew through the late 2010s to a pre-COVID peak of 3,395 in AY 2018—19 (index 137.2 vs. AY 2015—16 = 100), before COVID knocked them down to 2,874 (index 116.1). The much-touted +13.1% YoY jump in AY 2023—24 (3,041 → 3,438) lifts the index only to 138.9 — barely above the pre-pandemic peak, closer to a return to the pre-COVID trend than a GPT-induced surge. Source: CRA Taulbee Survey, Table 1 [S8–S11, S1–S5].

Three Years After the GPT Moment -- A Subtle Decline

Total Fall enrollment headcount, indexed to each school's earliest available year (= 100). Coverage is limited to schools whose own institutional-research pages publish multi-year CS enrollment.

Bachelor's — the surge has clearly rolled over at most tracked schools as AY 2025–26 numbers arrive: Michigan State −27% (2,087 → 1,527), Georgia Tech −18% (4,681 → 3,860), Stony Brook −15% (1,129 → 954), UMass Amherst −8% (1,590 → 1,470), and even UW–Madison −3.6% (2,606 → 2,513) off their respective peaks. Only CMU (latest data AY 2024–25) is still clearly climbing.

Master's — residential programs are softening on a similar curve: UMass −7% YoY and UW–Madison −27% off its AY 2022–23 peak. Georgia Tech is the loud exception, but its master's line is dominated by OMSCS (the online program scaling to 19,035 students in AY 2025–26), not residential demand.

PhD — enrollment is still growing at almost every tracked school through AY 2025–26 (UMass is the lone exception, −9% YoY). The break is bachelor's-led, with residential master's following on the same curve, while research-track demand looks unaffected so far.
